Together, these outputs form a critical foundation for all subsequent phases of the Qeet ID build.

### 7. Interview Findings — By Stakeholder Group

---

### 7.1 Executive Leadership

*(CEO / Founder, Finance Lead)*

**Key Findings:**

- The CEO sees Qeet ID as a strategic anchor product for Qeet Group — a platform that can serve all 10 subsidiaries internally while generating external revenue
- Primary concern is speed to market — the CEO wants Qeet ID live within 15 months maximum, with no timeline slippage
- The board expects a clear path to $1M ARR within 12 months of launch
- The Finance Lead raised concerns about infrastructure cost overruns — wants monthly cloud spend visibility from Day 1
- Budget has been approved in principle but requires a formal cost breakdown before Phase 2 begins
- The CEO wants Qeet ID to be self-sufficient financially within 24 months of launch
- Both stakeholders strongly support the developer-first go-to-market strategy but want enterprise revenue prioritized from Month 6 post-launch

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Concern about competing with well-funded incumbents like Okta and Auth0 without a significant marketing budget
- Uncertainty about hiring timeline for senior engineering roles

**Expectations:**

- Monthly steering committee updates with clear milestone tracking
- Financial dashboard with real-time budget vs actual spend visibility
- Clear risk register maintained and updated by Product Manager

**Priority Features from This Group:**

- Multi-tenancy (serves all Qeet Group subsidiaries)
- Enterprise SSO and SAML (drives enterprise revenue)
- Billing and subscription management (enables self-serve revenue)

---

### 7.2 Technology Leadership

*(CTO, Solution Architect, Backend Engineering Lead, Frontend Engineering Lead, DevOps / SRE Lead)*

**Key Findings:**

- The CTO strongly advocates for a microservices architecture from Day 1 to ensure scalability — monolith is not on the table
- The preferred cloud provider is AWS, with multi-region deployment planned for Year 2
- The Backend Engineering Lead flagged that building a fully compliant OAuth 2.0 and SAML engine from scratch is a 4–6 month effort for the core team — timeline must reflect this
- Strong consensus that Kubernetes is the right orchestration platform — team has existing expertise
- The DevOps Lead raised a critical concern — CI/CD pipeline and infrastructure as code must be set up before development begins, not after
- The Solution Architect recommends using an existing open-source identity foundation (such as Keycloak or Ory) as a base layer, then building Qeet ID's proprietary experience on top — this could reduce MVP development time by 3–4 months
- Frontend Lead confirmed the admin dashboard and developer portal are equally important as the core auth engine — both must be production-quality at launch
- All technical stakeholders agreed that test coverage must be a non-negotiable standard — minimum 80% before any production deployment

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Concern about hiring senior backend engineers with identity protocol expertise — this is a specialized skill set
- Risk of technical debt if architecture decisions are rushed in early phases
- DevOps Lead flagged that infrastructure costs could spike without proper auto-scaling and cost monitoring from the start

**Expectations:**

- Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) for all major technical decisions
- Clear API design standards agreed before development begins
- Weekly engineering syncs between CTO, Solution Architect, and all Engineering Leads

**Priority Features from This Group:**

- Microservices architecture with clear service boundaries
- OAuth 2.0, OIDC, SAML 2.0 as MVP non-negotiables
- Kubernetes-based infrastructure with auto-scaling
- CI/CD pipeline with automated testing gates
- Comprehensive API documentation

### 7.3 Security & Compliance

*(Security Architect, CISO, Compliance Officer)*

**Key Findings:**

- The CISO was unequivocal — SOC 2 Type I must be achieved before a single production customer is onboarded. This is a hard constraint, not a preference
- GDPR compliance requires data residency controls — customers must be able to choose where their identity data is stored (EU vs US at minimum)
- The Security Architect recommends a Zero Trust architecture from Day 1 — retrofitting security into an existing architecture is always more expensive and risky
- The Compliance Officer flagged that HIPAA compliance will be required if Qeet ID targets healthcare customers — this should be planned for in v1.5
- All three stakeholders strongly recommended engaging a third-party penetration testing firm before beta launch — not after
- The CISO wants a formal Security Development Lifecycle (SDL) process adopted by the engineering team from the start of Phase 4
- Encryption at rest and in transit is mandatory for all user data — AES-256 and TLS 1.3 minimum
- Audit logs must be immutable, tamper-proof, and retained for a minimum of 12 months
- The Compliance Officer raised the need for Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) to be ready before any enterprise pilot customer is onboarded

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Concern that engineering timelines will deprioritize security controls in favor of feature delivery
- Uncertainty about which third-party auditor to engage for SOC 2 — needs early selection
- GDPR data deletion requirements are complex to implement correctly in an identity platform — needs dedicated engineering effort

**Expectations:**

- Security Architect to be embedded in Phase 2 architecture design from Day 1
- Weekly security review meetings throughout Phases 4 and 5
- Penetration testing scheduled and budgeted before Phase 6 begins
- Compliance Officer to own and maintain the Compliance Requirements Matrix throughout all phases

**Priority Features from This Group:**

- Zero Trust architecture
- AES-256 encryption at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit
- Immutable audit logs with 12-month retention
- GDPR data export and deletion tools
- Rate limiting and bot detection
- SOC 2 Type I controls implemented in Phase 4

---

### 7.4 Legal & Finance

*(Legal Counsel, Finance Lead)*

**Key Findings:**

- Legal Counsel confirmed that Qeet ID's legal entity as a standalone subsidiary must be formally incorporated before any customer contracts are signed
- IP protection for the core authentication engine must be assessed — trade secret protection is preferred over patent filing for speed and cost
- Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) must be templated and reviewed by legal before enterprise pilot onboarding begins
- The Finance Lead wants a detailed cost model for infrastructure before cloud provider selection is finalized
- Finance confirmed the contingency budget is set at 15% of total project cost
- Legal raised a concern about open-source license compatibility if the team uses Keycloak or Ory as a base layer — GPL vs Apache 2.0 implications must be reviewed
- Legal wants to review all third-party SDK and API integrations before they go into production
- Finance wants monthly budget vs actual reporting with variance explanations

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Open-source license risk if using existing identity frameworks as a foundation
- Data handling agreements for enterprise customers are complex and time-consuming to negotiate
- Concern about liability exposure if a security breach occurs during the beta phase

**Expectations:**

- Legal entity incorporation completed by end of Phase 1
- DPA template ready before Phase 8 beta launch
- Monthly financial reporting dashboard accessible to Finance Lead

**Priority Deliverables from This Group:**

- Legal entity formation document
- IP protection strategy
- Open-source license compatibility review
- DPA template
- Monthly budget reporting framework

### 7.5 Design & User Experience

*(UI/UX Designer)*

**Key Findings:**

- The UX Designer strongly emphasized that the developer onboarding experience is the single most important design challenge — if a developer cannot complete their first integration in under 10 minutes, Qeet ID will lose them permanently
- The admin dashboard must support both technical and non-technical users — IT admins should not need to write code to manage users and roles
- The login and authentication UI components (login box, MFA screens, passwordless flows) must be fully customizable — white-label capability is non-negotiable for enterprise customers
- The designer flagged that accessibility is often deprioritized in identity platforms — Qeet ID should target WCAG 2.1 AA compliance at launch
- Mobile-first design is essential for authentication flows — the majority of end-user logins happen on mobile devices
- The designer recommended building a component library and design system before any frontend development begins — to ensure consistency across all Qeet ID surfaces
- Dark mode support should be included from launch — it is now a standard expectation among developer audiences

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Concern that engineering will begin frontend development before the design system is finalized
- Risk of inconsistent UI patterns across the admin dashboard, developer portal, and authentication flows if design is not centralized

**Expectations:**

- Design system and component library completed before Phase 4 frontend development begins
- Weekly design review sessions with Frontend Engineering Lead throughout Phase 4
- Usability testing with real developers before beta launch

**Priority Design Deliverables:**

- Developer onboarding flow (signup to first auth in under 10 minutes)
- Customizable authentication UI components
- Admin dashboard for non-technical users
- Mobile-first authentication flows
- WCAG 2.1 AA accessible components
- Dark mode support

### 7.6 Sales & Marketing

*(Sales Lead, Marketing Lead, Developer Relations Lead)*

**Key Findings:**

- The Sales Lead confirmed that enterprise deals will be blocked without SOC 2 Type I — this validates the compliance team's hard requirement
- The top objection enterprise buyers raise when evaluating new auth platforms is vendor lock-in risk — Qeet ID must communicate a clear migration and data portability story
- The Marketing Lead recommends a developer-first content strategy — technical blog posts, tutorials, and open-source contributions will drive organic awareness more effectively than paid advertising in this market
- Developer Relations Lead emphasized that being active on GitHub, Discord, and developer communities (Dev.to, Hacker News) is essential from Day 1 of beta launch
- The Sales Lead wants a clear competitive battle card against Okta, Auth0, and Microsoft Entra ID ready before enterprise sales conversations begin
- Marketing confirmed that the Qeet Group brand gives Qeet ID immediate credibility as a subsidiary of a diversified conglomerate — this is a differentiator versus standalone auth startups
- The Developer Relations Lead flagged that a generous free tier (10,000 MAUs) is a critical competitive advantage — it must be prominently featured in all marketing materials
- All three stakeholders agreed that launching on Product Hunt, GitHub, and developer newsletters simultaneously on launch day is the highest-impact go-to-market tactic

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Concern that Qeet ID will be invisible to the developer market without early community investment before launch
- Risk of enterprise sales cycle being longer than projected — 3–6 months is typical for identity platform deals
- Marketing budget may be insufficient to compete with Okta and Auth0's brand awareness

**Expectations:**

- Competitive battle cards ready 4 weeks before beta launch
- Developer community presence established during beta phase
- Launch plan finalized and approved 6 weeks before production launch

**Priority Deliverables from This Group:**

- Competitive battle cards (Okta, Auth0, Microsoft Entra ID, Firebase)
- Developer content strategy and editorial calendar
- Go-to-market launch plan
- Free tier positioning and messaging
- Partnership outreach to AWS Marketplace and GitHub Marketplace

### 7.7 Customer Success & Support

*(Customer Success Lead)*

**Key Findings:**

- The Customer Success Lead identified that onboarding is where most auth platforms lose customers — the first 7 days of a developer's experience are critical to long-term retention
- Enterprise customers expect a dedicated Customer Success Manager (CSM) from Day 1 of their contract — this must be budgeted for in the enterprise pricing tier
- Support SLA expectations vary significantly by tier — free tier users expect community support, growth tier expects email support within 24 hours, enterprise expects dedicated support with a 4-hour response SLA
- The Customer Success Lead wants an in-product guided onboarding flow — not just documentation — that walks developers step-by-step through their first integration
- Proactive health monitoring is essential — Qeet ID should alert customers before they hit usage limits or experience potential issues, not after
- A customer feedback loop must be embedded from Day 1 — in-app feedback widgets, NPS surveys at key milestones, and regular quarterly business reviews for enterprise customers

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Concern that support volume will spike at launch without adequate team capacity
- Risk of enterprise customers churning if they do not feel valued post-onboarding
- Lack of in-product onboarding guidance is a common failure point in competing platforms

**Expectations:**

- Customer Success team hired and trained before beta launch
- Support tiers clearly defined and communicated on pricing page
- In-product onboarding flow designed in Phase 3 and built in Phase 4

**Priority Deliverables from This Group:**

- In-product guided onboarding flow
- Support tier SLA definitions
- Customer health monitoring dashboard
- NPS survey integration at key user milestones
- Enterprise QBR (Quarterly Business Review) framework

### 7.8 Beta & Enterprise Pilot Customers

*(3 Beta Customer Representatives, 2 Enterprise Pilot Representatives)*

**Key Findings:**

**Beta Customers (Developers / Startups):**

- All three representatives cited poor documentation as their biggest frustration with existing auth platforms — they want real code examples, not abstract API references
- Time to first working integration is the defining moment — if it takes more than 30 minutes, they look elsewhere
- Pricing transparency is critical — hidden costs at scale are a major trust-breaker
- They want a sandbox environment that is fully functional and free — not a limited demo
- GitHub-based SDK repositories with active maintenance signals are important trust indicators

**Enterprise Pilot Customers:**

- Both enterprise representatives confirmed that their security team will conduct an independent security review before approving any identity platform — SOC 2 Type I and GDPR compliance are minimum entry requirements
- They want SAML SSO and SCIM provisioning working flawlessly before sign-off — these are non-negotiable for enterprise IT integration
- Custom domain support for authentication pages is required — they cannot present a Qeet ID-branded login page to their employees
- SLA guarantees with financial penalties for downtime are expected in enterprise contracts
- Both representatives expressed strong interest in Qeet ID's multi-subsidiary positioning within Qeet Group — they see it as a signal of long-term commitment and stability

**Pain Points Identified:**

- Current auth platforms are either too developer-focused (lacking enterprise features) or too enterprise-focused (too complex for developers) — Qeet ID's dual positioning is seen as a genuine gap in the market
- Pricing unpredictability at scale is a recurring complaint about Auth0
- SAML configuration is notoriously complex in competing platforms — simpler SAML setup would be a strong differentiator

**Expectations:**

- Fully functional sandbox environment available from first day of beta
- Real-world code examples in all major languages in documentation
- SAML and SCIM working flawlessly before enterprise pilot sign-off
- Custom domain support for authentication pages
- Transparent, predictable pricing published on the website

### 8. Cross-Stakeholder Themes & Patterns

The following themes emerged consistently across multiple stakeholder groups during interviews:

---

### Theme 1 — Developer Experience is the #1 Priority

Mentioned by: CTO, UI/UX Designer, Developer Relations, Beta Customers, Customer Success

Every stakeholder group independently identified developer experience as the most critical success factor. Time to first auth, SDK quality, and documentation depth were cited repeatedly as the make-or-break factors for developer adoption.

**Implication:** Qeet ID must invest equally in developer experience as it does in core auth engine development. A dedicated SDK engineering team and technical writer must be resourced from Phase 4.

---

### Theme 2 — Compliance is a Hard Gate, Not a Nice-to-Have

Mentioned by: CEO, CISO, Security Architect, Compliance Officer, Legal, Sales, Enterprise Pilot Customers

SOC 2 Type I and GDPR compliance were mentioned as non-negotiable by every stakeholder group that touches enterprise customers. No enterprise will onboard without verified compliance.

**Implication:** Compliance must be built into the architecture from Phase 2 — not retrofitted after development. The SOC 2 audit process must begin in Phase 5 to ensure certification before Phase 9 production launch.

---

### Theme 3 — Pricing Transparency Builds Trust

Mentioned by: Marketing, Sales, Beta Customers, Enterprise Pilot Customers

Competitors like Auth0 have damaged trust through unpredictable pricing at scale. Qeet ID has an opportunity to differentiate through transparent, predictable pricing that developers and enterprises can plan around.

**Implication:** Pricing model must be finalized and published on the website from Day 1 of beta launch. No hidden costs or ambiguous usage limits.

---

### Theme 4 — Multi-Tenancy is Non-Negotiable

Mentioned by: CEO, CTO, Solution Architect, Enterprise Pilot Customers

Both internal (serving Qeet Group subsidiaries) and external (serving enterprise customers with multiple organizations) use cases demand robust multi-tenancy from the start.

**Implication:** Multi-tenancy must be a core architectural requirement in Phase 2 — not an afterthought. Every design decision must account for tenant isolation and data separation.

---

### Theme 5 — The Qeet Group Brand is an Advantage

Mentioned by: CEO, Marketing, Sales, Enterprise Pilot Customers

Being a subsidiary of a diversified, multi-industry conglomerate gives Qeet ID credibility that standalone auth startups cannot claim. Enterprise customers see long-term commitment and financial stability.

**Implication:** All Qeet ID marketing and sales materials should prominently reference the Qeet Group backing. The parent company story should be woven into the enterprise sales narrative.

### 11. Recommendations from Business Analyst

Based on all interview findings, the following recommendations are made to the Product Manager and CTO for consideration before Phase 2 begins:

**Recommendation 1 — Evaluate open-source base layer immediately**
The Solution Architect's suggestion to use Keycloak or Ory as a foundation is high-value. Legal must complete the license audit in Week 4 to unblock the architecture decision.

**Recommendation 2 — Begin hiring senior identity engineers in parallel with Phase 1**
The market for engineers with OAuth 2.0, SAML, and SCIM expertise is competitive. Waiting until Phase 2 to begin hiring will create a bottleneck. HR and CTO should begin outreach immediately.

**Recommendation 3 — Engage SOC 2 auditor before Phase 2 ends**
SOC 2 Type I requires 3–6 months of evidence collection before the audit. Engaging the auditor early ensures the engineering team knows exactly which controls to implement from Phase 4.

**Recommendation 4 — Lock pricing model before beta launch**
Pricing transparency is a top theme across stakeholder groups. The pricing model must be finalized and approved by Product Manager, Finance, and Sales before beta launch — not after.

**Recommendation 5 — Establish developer community presence during beta**
Developer Relations must begin building community presence (Discord, GitHub, Dev.to) from the first day of beta. Waiting for production launch to start community building is too late.

**Recommendation 6 — Build in-product onboarding from Phase 4**
Customer Success and UX Designer both flagged this. In-product guided onboarding is as important as documentation. It must be scoped into Phase 4 development — not deferred to post-launch.
